🔒 Privacy Policy
Last updated: March 2026 · Plain English. No legal jargon.

The short version: Anchor collects nothing. All data you enter stays on your device only. We never see it, never store it, never share it. You are in complete control at all times.

1. Who We Are
Anchor Safety is a free emergency safety application designed to help individuals and families prepare for and respond to emergencies. Anchor Safety is provided free of charge to individuals worldwide. Our mission is to make emergency safety accessible to every human being on earth regardless of language, ability, or location.
Contact: [email protected] · anchorsafety.org · Auckland, New Zealand
2. What Data We Collect

We collect NO personal data. Anchor does not operate any servers that receive or store your personal information.

All information you choose to enter into Anchor — including your name, phone number, emergency contacts, family member details, and location — is stored exclusively on your own device using your device's local storage. This data never leaves your device and is never transmitted to Anchor or any third party.

3. Location Data
Anchor only accesses your GPS location when you explicitly take one of the following actions: opening the Safety Map, tapping the SOS button, or tapping the Share Location button. Your location is never accessed in the background, never tracked continuously, and never stored or transmitted to Anchor.
When you share your location via SOS, the coordinates are sent directly from your device to your chosen emergency contacts via SMS. Anchor does not receive or log this information.
4. Third Party Services
Anchor uses the following third party services to display maps and locate hospitals. These services have their own privacy policies:
• OpenStreetMap / Overpass API — used to display maps and find nearby hospitals. Your approximate location is sent to this service only when you open the map. No personal data is included.
• CartoDB — provides the map tile images you see on screen. No personal data is shared.
Anchor does not use Google Analytics, Facebook Pixel, advertising networks, or any other tracking or analytics services.
5. Your Rights
Under the New Zealand Privacy Act 2020, GDPR (Europe), and equivalent laws worldwide, you have the right to access, correct, and delete your personal data at any time.
Since all data is stored only on your device, you have complete control at all times. To delete all data: open Settings and tap "Clear All Data." This permanently removes everything instantly.
6. Children
Anchor is designed to help protect children in emergencies. We do not knowingly collect any personal information from children. All information relating to children entered into Anchor is entered by a parent or guardian on their own device and is never transmitted to Anchor.
7. Changes to This Policy
If we ever change this privacy policy we will notify users through the app. We will never reduce your privacy protections without your explicit consent. Our core promise — that we collect nothing and your data stays on your device — will never change.
8. Contact
If you have any questions about this privacy policy please contact us at [email protected]. We are committed to responding to all privacy inquiries within 5 business days.

Our final promise to you: Anchor was built to save lives — not to profit from your data. We will never sell, share, or monetise your personal information. Ever. Your trust is worth more than any amount of money.

🩺 First Aid Guide
Step-by-step emergency instructions · Works offline
🔍
❤️
CPR — Adult
Cardiac arrest · Not breathing
›
1
Call emergency services NOW
Call 111 (NZ) or your local emergency number before starting CPR if someone else is present.
2
Check the scene is safe
Make sure you and the person are not in danger before approaching.
3
Check for response
Tap shoulders firmly and shout "Are you okay?" If no response, begin CPR.
4
30 chest compressions
Place heel of hand on centre of chest. Push down hard and fast — 5–6 cm deep. Rate: 100–120 per minute (Stayin' Alive beat).
5
2 rescue breaths
Tilt head back, lift chin. Pinch nose, seal mouth, blow for 1 second. Watch chest rise. Repeat twice.
6
Continue 30:2 ratio
Keep going — 30 compressions, 2 breaths — until help arrives or person recovers.
⚠️ If not trained in rescue breaths, do hands-only CPR — continuous compressions only, no breaths.
😮
Choking — Adult
Cannot breathe · Coughing · Turning blue
›
1
Encourage coughing
If they can cough, encourage them to keep coughing forcefully. Do not interfere.
2
5 back blows
Lean them forward. Give 5 firm blows between shoulder blades with heel of your hand.
3
5 abdominal thrusts
Stand behind them. Make a fist above navel. Pull sharply inward and upward 5 times.
4
Repeat until clear
Alternate 5 back blows and 5 abdominal thrusts until object is dislodged or they become unconscious.
5
If unconscious — start CPR
Lower them carefully to the ground and begin CPR immediately. Call emergency services.
🏚️
Earthquake
During and after shaking
›
1
DROP, COVER, HOLD ON
Drop to hands and knees. Take cover under a sturdy table or desk. Hold on and protect your head and neck.
2
Stay inside until shaking stops
Do NOT run outside. Most injuries happen when people try to move during shaking.
3
After shaking — check for injuries
Check yourself and others for injuries. Apply first aid. Do not move seriously injured people unless in immediate danger.
4
Check for hazards
Gas leaks (smell), damaged wiring, structural damage. If gas leak — open windows, leave immediately, do not use electrical switches.
5
Expect aftershocks
Move to open ground away from buildings, trees, and power lines. Be prepared for aftershocks.
⚠️ If near coast — move to high ground immediately after shaking stops. Tsunami risk is real.
🌊
Flooding
Flash floods · Rising water
›
1
Move to higher ground immediately
Do not wait. Take essential items only. Move children and elderly first.
2
Never walk in moving water
Just 15cm of moving water can knock you down. 30cm of water can sweep a car away.
3
Turn off utilities
Turn off electricity at the main switch if it is safe to do so. Do not touch electrical equipment in wet areas.
4
If trapped indoors
Move to the highest floor. Signal for help from windows. Do not go to the roof unless water is rising to your level.
5
After flooding
Do not return until authorities say it is safe. Avoid floodwater — it may be contaminated. Document damage for insurance.
🩸
Severe Bleeding
Deep cuts · Wounds · Heavy blood loss
›
1
Apply direct pressure
Press firmly on the wound with a clean cloth, bandage, or clothing. Do not remove — add more material on top if soaked through.
2
Elevate the injured area
Raise the injured limb above the level of the heart if possible. This slows blood flow.
3
Keep pressing for 10 minutes
Do not lift the cloth to check. Maintain constant firm pressure for at least 10 minutes without releasing.
4
If bleeding doesn't stop
For limb wounds: apply a tourniquet 5–7 cm above the wound. Tighten until bleeding stops. Note the time applied.
5
Treat for shock
Lay person down, elevate legs, keep warm. Do not give food or water. Call emergency services immediately.
🔥
Fire — Escape Plan
Building fire · Smoke · Escape
›
1
Get out immediately
Do not stop to collect belongings. Every second counts. Alert others as you leave.
2
Stay low in smoke
Smoke rises. Crawl low where air is cleaner. Cover nose and mouth with wet cloth if possible.
3
Test doors before opening
Touch door with back of hand. If hot — do not open. Use another exit. If cool — open slowly and check for smoke.
4
If trapped — signal for help
Close doors between you and fire. Seal gaps with clothing. Signal from window. Call emergency services with your location.
5
Meet at your family meeting point
Go to your pre-agreed meeting point. Never re-enter a burning building for any reason.
👶
Choking — Infant under 1
Baby cannot breathe · Turning blue
›
1
Face-down back blows
Hold baby face-down along your forearm, head lower than chest. Give 5 gentle but firm back blows between shoulder blades with 2 fingers.
2
Face-up chest thrusts
Turn baby face-up on your forearm. Give 5 chest thrusts using 2 fingers on centre of chest, just below nipple line.
3
Check mouth
Look in mouth — only remove object if you can clearly see it. Never do a blind finger sweep.
4
Repeat until clear
Alternate back blows and chest thrusts. If baby becomes unconscious, begin infant CPR and call emergency services.
⚠️ Never use abdominal thrusts on an infant under 1 year old — use chest thrusts only.
🔥
Burns
Heat · Chemical · Electrical burns
›
1
Cool the burn immediately
Run cool (not cold or iced) water over the burn for at least 20 minutes. Do not use ice, butter, or toothpaste.
2
Remove clothing and jewellery
Remove from the burned area only if not stuck to the skin. Do not pull off stuck material.
3
Cover the burn loosely
Use a clean non-fluffy material — cling film or a clean plastic bag is ideal. Do not wrap tightly.
4
Seek medical help
Get medical help for any burn larger than a postage stamp, on face/hands/joints, or in children and elderly.
⚠️ For chemical burns — remove contaminated clothing, flush with large amounts of water for 20+ minutes. Call emergency services.
🌋
Volcanic Eruption
Ash fall · Lava · Toxic gases
›
1
Evacuate immediately if ordered
Follow official evacuation orders without delay. Take your go-bag. Do not wait to see what happens.
2
Protect from ash fall
Wear a P2/N95 mask or fold a damp cloth over nose and mouth. Wear goggles to protect eyes. Cover all skin — long sleeves, gloves.
3
Stay indoors if ash is falling
Close all windows and doors. Seal gaps with wet towels. Turn off air conditioning and fans. Bring animals inside.
4
Protect your water supply
Cover water tanks and rainwater collection. Do not drink tap water until authorities say it is safe. Ash contaminates water supplies.
5
Clear ash from roofs carefully
Wet ash is extremely heavy and can collapse roofs. Clear carefully using a brush — not a hose. Wear full protection.
6
Watch for lahars
Lahars are fast-moving volcanic mudflows triggered by rain or melting snow. Move to high ground if near rivers or valleys after eruption.
⚠️ Volcanic ash is razor sharp glass particles — it damages lungs permanently. Always wear a mask. Even light ash fall is dangerous.
⚠️
Gas Leak
Natural gas · LPG · Carbon monoxide
›
1
Do NOT switch anything on or off
Do not touch light switches, appliances, doorbells or phones. Any spark can ignite gas. Leave immediately.
2
Open windows and doors as you leave
If safe to do so, open windows and doors to ventilate as you exit. Do not stop to collect belongings.
3
Turn off gas at the meter if safe
Only if the meter is outside and you can reach it without going back inside. Turn the valve 90 degrees to close.
4
Move far away and call for help
Move at least 100 metres away. Call emergency services and your gas provider from outside. Do not re-enter until cleared.
5
Carbon monoxide — silent killer
You cannot smell or see CO. Symptoms: headache, dizziness, nausea. Get fresh air immediately. Call 111. Install a CO detector in your home.
⚠️ Never use generators, BBQs, or gas appliances indoors — even in the garage. Carbon monoxide kills silently within minutes.
🦠
Epidemic / Disease Outbreak
Infectious disease · Pandemic · Quarantine
›
1
Follow official health guidance
Listen only to official government and health authority sources. Avoid misinformation. Follow quarantine and isolation instructions.
2
Wear a mask correctly
Use a surgical mask or P2/N95 in public. Pinch the nose wire, cover nose and mouth fully. Change every 4 hours or when damp.
3
Hand hygiene — most important step
Wash hands with soap for 20 seconds frequently. Use hand sanitiser when soap unavailable. Do not touch your face.
4
Isolate the sick
Keep sick family members in a separate room if possible. Dedicated bathroom if available. Wear mask when caring for them. Open windows for ventilation.
5
Stock up carefully
Keep 2 weeks of food, water, and essential medications. Include oral rehydration salts, fever medication, and extra masks and gloves.
6
Protect the most vulnerable
Elderly, disabled, and immunocompromised people need extra protection. Check on neighbours. Use your Care Circle to coordinate support.
⚠️ Seek medical help immediately if someone shows severe symptoms — difficulty breathing, persistent chest pain, confusion, or cannot stay awake.

💙 How Care Circle Works
1
Add vulnerable neighbours — elderly, disabled, families with young children, people living alone — to your Care Circle.
2
During an emergency Anchor automatically sends a check-in request to everyone in your circle.
3
If someone doesn't check in within 1 hour you get an alert. You go check on them personally or send help.
4
No one in your neighbourhood is forgotten. No one is left behind. Every person matters.
5
Works via satellite when cell towers are down. Your Care Circle stays connected no matter what.

How to Make a Fire
Warmth · Cooking · Signalling for help
›
1
FIND A SAFE SPOT
Clear flat ground. No dry grass nearby. No trees overhead. At least 3 metres from your tent. Make a ring of rocks to contain the fire.
2
GATHER 3 TYPES OF FUEL
🌿 TINDER — dry leaves, paper, bark, grass. Lights easily. 🌿 KINDLING — dry sticks, finger-thick. 🪵 WOOD — dry logs, arm-thick. Collect more than you think you need.
3
BUILD A TENT SHAPE
Put tinder in the centre. Lean kindling sticks around it like a tent. Leave one gap facing the wind for air. Add larger wood on the outside.
4
LIGHT FROM THE BOTTOM
Use waterproof matches or lighter. Light the tinder at the base of the gap. Cup your hands to block wind. Blow gently and steadily at the base.
5
FEED IT SLOWLY
Add small sticks first. Then bigger wood when fire is strong. Never dump a big load at once — you will smother it. A steady small fire is safer than a big one.
6
PUT IT OUT PROPERLY
Pour water over fire. Stir the ash. Pour more water. Touch the ash — if warm, add more water. Fire must be stone cold before you sleep or leave.
⚠️ NEVER leave fire unattended. NEVER make fire in strong wind. ALWAYS have water nearby. NEVER make fire near your fuel supplies.
💡 Best fire starters in order — 1) Lighter 2) Waterproof matches 3) Flint and steel — strike downward onto tinder, sparks at 3000°C. 4) Focus sunlight through glasses or water-filled plastic bag onto tinder. Always carry flint — it never runs out.

Fire Safety With Children
Rules every child must know before any fire is lit
›
1
DRAW THE SAFETY CIRCLE
Mark a circle 2 big adult steps from the fire using rope, stones or a line in the dirt. Tell every child — NEVER cross this line. Review it every time before lighting.
2
ONE ADULT ALWAYS WATCHES
One adult watches fire AND children at ALL times. No exceptions. If you must leave — put the fire completely out first. Never leave older children in charge of fire.
3
TEACH STOP DROP ROLL NOW
Practise BEFORE any emergency. If clothes catch fire — STOP immediately. DROP to the ground. ROLL slowly to smother flames. Cover face with hands. Practise this today.
4
NO LOOSE CLOTHING
Tie back all long hair. Roll up loose sleeves. No scarves or dangling clothes near fire. Synthetic fabrics melt into skin — wear cotton near fire.
5
WATER READY ALWAYS
Full bucket of water beside fire at all times. If burned — run cool water over burn for 20 minutes. Do not use ice. Do not pop blisters. Seek medical help.
⚠️ Children under 5 must be held or secured at all times near any fire. Never leave them unattended even for one minute.
💡 Make fire safety a game before emergencies — children who practise respond better in real situations.

Surviving Extreme Heat
Heatwave · Desert · Hot evacuation
›
1
DRINK BEFORE YOU ARE THIRSTY
Thirst means you are already dehydrated. Small sips constantly. Adults 1 litre per hour in extreme heat. Check children and elderly first — they dehydrate faster and show signs later.
2
REST FROM 10AM TO 4PM
These are the killing hours. Find shade and stay still. Move only at dawn or after sunset. Walking in midday heat burns energy and water dangerously fast.
3
COVER ALL SKIN
Long sleeves and wide brim hat with neck cover. Light colours reflect heat. This feels hotter but keeps you cooler — it blocks direct sun on your skin which generates enormous heat.
4
MAKE SHADE IF NONE EXISTS
Use your emergency tent, tarpaulin or jacket propped on sticks. Even a small shadow saves lives. Stay off hot ground — use a mat or bag as insulation.
5
HEAT STROKE — ACT IMMEDIATELY
Signs — hot dry skin, no sweating, confusion, fast pulse, red face. This kills fast. Move to shade. Remove excess clothing. Wet their skin. Fan them. Call emergency services NOW.
💡 Wet a cloth and place on neck, wrists and forehead — these pulse points cool the whole body rapidly.
⚠️ Never give alcohol in heat — it causes dehydration. Never give very cold water to heat stroke victim — causes shock. Cool slowly.
❄️
Surviving Extreme Cold
Winter · Snow · Cold night evacuation
›
1
STAY DRY — THIS IS MOST IMPORTANT
Wet kills faster than cold. If clothes are wet — change immediately even if cold. Wet cotton is deadly. Wool and synthetic fabrics keep you warm even when wet.
2
THREE LAYERS
Base layer — traps warmth next to skin. Middle layer — insulates. Outer layer — blocks wind and rain. Cover head always — 40% of body heat escapes from the head.
3
GET OFF THE GROUND
Cold ground pulls heat from your body very fast. Always sleep on a mat, branches, dry leaves or bags. Never directly on cold ground or snow — even for a short rest.
4
EAT AND DRINK WARM
Your body burns food to make heat. Eat regularly even if not hungry. Hot drinks warm from inside. Avoid alcohol — it feels warm but makes your body colder. Give children extra food.
5
HYPOTHERMIA — ACT IMMEDIATELY
Shivering STOPS — this is dangerous not good. Confusion. Slurred speech. Extreme sleepiness. Get them warm slowly — dry clothes, body heat, warm drinks. Call emergency services.
💡 Huddle together — body heat from others is the fastest way to rewarm someone with hypothermia. Use sleeping bags, blankets, coats together.
⚠️ Never rub frostbitten skin — causes tissue damage. Warm slowly in lukewarm water only. Never walk on frostbitten feet if avoidable.

Surviving a Volcanic Eruption
Ash · Lava · Toxic gas · Lahars
›
1
LEAVE IMMEDIATELY
Do not wait to see what happens. Volcanoes move faster than people expect. Take your go-bag. Drive away from the volcano — never towards it. Follow official evacuation routes.
2
COVER EVERY PART OF YOUR BODY
Ash is razor sharp glass particles. P2/N95 mask on face. Goggles on eyes. Long sleeves and gloves on all skin. Ash in lungs causes permanent damage. Ash in eyes causes blindness.
3
MOVE TO HIGH GROUND
Lava flows downhill. Toxic gas settles in valleys. Lahars follow rivers at high speed. Always move UP and AWAY from rivers, valleys and low ground.
4
SEAL YOUR SHELTER
Close all windows and doors. Seal every gap with wet towels. Turn off fans and air conditioning. Stay inside until ash completely stops falling. Do not go outside for any reason.
5
CLEAR ASH FROM ROOF CAREFULLY
Wet ash is extremely heavy and collapses roofs. Brush with a broom from the roof edge. Do NOT hose it — wet ash doubles in weight. Wear full protection. Work slowly and carefully.
⚠️ A lahar travels at 60km/h. If you hear a deep roar from a valley or river — RUN UP immediately. Do not try to outrun it on flat ground. You cannot.
💡 Cover water tanks and food supplies with plastic sheeting immediately. Volcanic ash contaminates water and food within minutes.

Finding Safe Water
When taps don't work · After any disaster
›
1
USE YOUR LIFESTRAW FIRST
Push LifeStraw into any water source and drink. Filters 99.9999% of bacteria instantly. No preparation. No boiling. Fastest safest method in any emergency.
2
BOIL FOR 1 FULL MINUTE
Rolling boil for 1 minute kills everything. At high altitude — boil 3 minutes. Let cool. Do not blow into it to cool — adds bacteria. Cover while cooling.
3
COLLECT RAIN WATER
Rain is safe to drink directly. Collect in any clean container. Stretch your emergency tent or tarpaulin as a funnel into a bucket. Fastest and safest water source in wet climates.
4
FIND MOVING WATER
Moving water is safer than still water. Follow animals — they always know where water is. Green vegetation means water nearby. Morning dew on leaves is safe to drink.
5
DEHYDRATION SIGNS — ACT NOW
Dark yellow urine means drink immediately. Dry mouth. Dizziness. Confusion. Children and elderly show signs faster. Give them water first always. Dehydration kills in 3 days.
💡 Dig 1 metre from a river bank — filtered water seeps through soil naturally. Safer than drinking directly from the river.
⚠️ After volcanic eruption — never drink tap water or river water until authorities confirm it is safe. Ash makes water toxic and unfilterble.

Building Emergency Shelter
When you have no building · Night · Bad weather
›
1
USE YOUR EMERGENCY TENT FIRST
Always use your go-bag emergency tent first. Set it up before dark. Choose flat dry ground. Away from rivers — flash floods come at night. Away from lone trees — lightning strikes them.
2
CHOOSE YOUR SPOT CAREFULLY
High and dry — not in a valley or low area. Natural windbreak behind you — a hill, rocks or thick bushes. Away from dead trees — they fall in wind. Check ground for ant nests.
3
INSULATE THE FLOOR
Put leaves, grass, branches or bags on the ground before sleeping. Cold and damp ground steals body heat very fast. Floor insulation is as important as the roof above you.
4
MAKE A LEAN-TO IF NO TENT
Find a fallen tree or 2 strong sticks. Lean branches at 45 degrees against it. Cover thickly with leaves from top down like roof tiles. Thicker than you think — at least arm-depth.
5
SMALL IS WARMER
A small shelter traps body heat better than a large one. Just big enough to lie in. Pack everyone together — shared body heat keeps everyone warmer through the night.
💡 A large plastic bag from your go-bag makes an emergency sleeping bag. Put legs inside the bag — traps body heat immediately.

Signalling for Help
When phones don't work · Being found faster
›
1
3 IS THE UNIVERSAL DISTRESS SIGNAL
3 whistle blasts. 3 fires in a triangle. 3 gunshots. 3 of anything. This is recognised worldwide as a call for help. Repeat every few minutes. Listen for a response.
2
MAKE YOURSELF VISIBLE FROM AIR
Lay out bright coloured items in an open clearing — clothing, bags, tarpaulin. Make a large X or SOS on the ground using rocks, logs or anything dark on light ground.
3
USE MIRROR OR REFLECTIVE SURFACE
Flash sunlight towards aircraft or rescuers using your Anchor hand signal mirror, phone screen, foil or reflective jacket. Aim by looking through the hole in the mirror at your target. Flash 3 short, 3 long, 3 short — this is SOS. Visible up to 50km on a clear day.
4
SMOKE SIGNALS IN DAYTIME
Dark smoke is visible for kilometres. Add green leaves or rubber to fire for dark smoke in daytime. Use white smoke — dry grass or cloth — at night when it shows against dark sky.
5
HEAD TORCH AT NIGHT
Flash your Anchor head torch in groups of 3. Reflective strips on your Anchor bag and headband are visible to rescuers with torches from hundreds of metres away.
💡 Stay in one place once you have signalled. Rescuers are coming to your last known location. Moving makes you harder to find.

Emergency Food Survival
When supplies run out · Foraging basics
›
1
RATION YOUR FOOD SUPPLY FIRST
Before foraging — ration what you have. Adults can survive 3 weeks without food but only 3 days without water. Prioritise water always. Eat less, drink more.
2
INSECTS ARE SAFE PROTEIN
Grubs, ants, grasshoppers — cook before eating. Remove wings and legs. High in protein. Avoid brightly coloured insects — bright colour means toxic. Avoid hairy caterpillars.
3
UNIVERSAL EDIBILITY TEST
If unsure about a plant — rub on wrist. Wait 15 minutes. If no reaction — touch to lips. Wait 15 minutes. If no reaction — small bite. Wait 8 hours. If no reaction — safe to eat.
4
SAFE TO EAT ANYWHERE ON EARTH
Dandelions — all parts edible. Grass seeds — cook like rice. Clover — all parts edible. Seaweed — rinse and eat raw or cook. Pine needles — boil for tea, high in vitamin C.
5
NEVER EAT THESE
Mushrooms unless 100% certain. Berries you cannot identify. Plants with milky white sap. Plants that smell of almonds — cyanide. Plants that sting or irritate skin.
⚠️ When in doubt — do not eat it. Hunger is uncomfortable. Poisoning can be fatal. Wait for rescue rather than risk unknown plants.
💡 Fishing requires no tools — bend a safety pin into a hook, use thread from clothing as line, use insects as bait. Rivers near disaster zones often have fish.
